Protect your digital life from ransomware

Clear, applicable tips for individuals and businesses. Use this guide to reduce risk, improve your digital habits, and organize basic defenses.

Personal

Every internet user is a potential ransomware target. An unprotected device is easy to attack. Education and layered controls drastically reduce risk.

1

VPN on public Wi-Fi

Avoid sensitive transactions on open networks. Use a trustworthy VPN if you can‚Äôt postpone the connection.

  • Prefer known networks or mobile tethering.
  • Sign out of banking and critical services.
  • Disable automatic connections to public Wi-Fi.
2

Security software

Keep antivirus/antimalware active and up to date; enable real-time protection and firewall.

  • Update the operating system and apps.
  • Enable anti-phishing filters in browser and email.
3

Trusted downloads

Prefer sites with HTTPS, trust seals, and a lock icon. Avoid suspicious links and attachments.

  • Be wary of unexpected image names or files.
  • Verify the sender‚Äôs domain and the real URL.
4

Strong passwords & MFA

Use unique, robust passwords (12‚Äì16+ characters) and complement them with MFA to stop the use of stolen credentials.

  • Length and complexity: uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols.
  • One password per service: avoid reuse across accounts.
  • Smart changes: rotate if there are signs of leaks or unusual access.
  • Strong MFA: TOTP, push, or FIDO2 keys (better than SMS).
  • Back up recovery codes in a safe place.
5

Password manager

Centralize and generate long, unique passwords automatically. Reduces the risk of weak and reused passwords.

  • Generate 16‚Äì24 character keys with true randomness.
  • Autofill with domain matching to avoid phishing.
  • Sync across devices and protect with the manager‚Äôs MFA.
6

Passkeys (FIDO2/WebAuthn)

Enable passkeys where available: phishing-resistant authentication that replaces or adds a layer to passwords.

  • Register at least two authenticators (phone and hardware key).
  • Store recovery keys and test sign-in.
  • Prioritize critical services: email, banking, and storage.
Quick tip: set monthly reminders to update apps and check your backups.

Complementary best practices

A

Backups

3-2-1 rule: three copies, two different media, one offline or in the cloud with immutable versioning.

B

Updates

Enable automatic updates for the OS and applications. Patching closes exploitable vulnerabilities.

Business

SMEs are frequent targets: fewer resources and processes, but valuable data. Implement defense in depth, microsegmentation, and strict privilege control.

1

SME vulnerability

Budget and staff constraints increase exposure to targeted and opportunistic ransomware campaigns.

  • Lower maturity in processes and incident response.
  • Gaps due to third-party dependencies.
2

Value of information

Customer, supplier, billing, and IP data are monetizable and targets for extortion.

  • Reputational damage and penalties.
  • Operational disruption and blackmail.
3

Microsegmentation

Divide the network into segments with their own policies and access to contain lateral movement.

  • Visibility of data flows.
  • Policies adaptable to app changes.
  • Aligned with Zero Trust.
Access control and Zero Trust: limiting privileges reduces the attack surface and the impact of intrusions. Zero Trust requires continuously verifying identity, device, and context before granting access.
A

Least privilege (PLP)

Grant only the access strictly necessary for the job function.

  • Role profiles (RBAC) and separation of duties.
  • Quarterly access reviews.
B

Zero Trust

Don‚Äôt trust by default: continuously validate identity and device posture.

  • Context: location, time, risk.
  • Conditional access and micro-authorization.
C

PAM (privileged access)

Manage and audit privileged accounts with a vault, rotation, and session recording.

  • Credentials in a vault with automatic rotation.
  • Session recording and alerts.
D

Remove local admin & JIT

Remove local administrator rights and use Just-In-Time elevation (temporary and approved).

  • Less persistence and impact from malware.
  • Automatic privilege expiration.
E

MFA for critical accounts

Require strong MFA (TOTP, push, or FIDO2) for admins, remote access, and sensitive data.

  • Blocks the use of stolen passwords.
  • Hardens VPN/SSH/RDP access.
F

Audit and review

Monitor access, generate immutable logs, and review permissions periodically.

  • Alerts for anomalous activity.
  • Access re-certification campaigns.

Quick checklist

Data backup

Data backup is the best way to protect against ransomware. A clean, isolated backup allows you to recover without paying ransoms.

1

Offline copies

Keep a secondary copy offline (e.g., tape or disconnected device) to avoid simultaneous encryption.

  • Physically disconnect after finishing the backup.
  • Store in a separate location.
2

Immutable storage

Use WORM (Write-Once-Read-Many) or object lock to prevent modifications and deletion of copies.

  • Retention policies and legal hold.
  • Protects against malicious deletion.
3

Protection on backup servers

Deploy EDR/antimalware on backup servers to detect and isolate encryption behavior.

  • Automatic blocking and quarantine.
  • Alerts for mass-encryption processes.
4

Backup frequency

For critical data, back up at least hourly. Adjust RPO/RTO based on business impact.

  • Frequent incrementals, scheduled fulls.
  • Prioritize databases and key systems.
5

3-2-1-1-0 rule

3 copies, on 2 types of media, 1 off-site, 1 immutable/offline, and 0 verified errors.

  • Automate integrity checks.
  • Ensure there are no errors in logs.
6

Separate network / Air-gap

Segment the backup network or use a logical/physical air-gap to isolate repositories from the rest of the environment.

  • Restricted access and allow-lists.
  • Disable unnecessary administrative paths.
7

Restore tests

Rehearse periodic restores (partial and full) to validate real timings and procedures.

  • Simulate ransomware scenarios.
  • Document steps and owners.
8

Encryption and keys

Encrypt backups in transit and at rest. Manage keys with KMS/HSM and scheduled rotation.

  • Separate keys from repositories.
  • Strict access control to the KMS.
9

Versioning and retention

Enable multiple versions and sufficient retention to prevent overwriting clean copies.

  • Retention tailored by criticality.
  • Avoid overly short rotation windows.
10

Monitoring and anomalies

Use alerts for anomalous spikes in changes/encryption and for backup job failures.

  • Real-time dashboards and notifications.
  • Integration with SIEM/SOAR.
Tip: document your recovery plan (RTO/RPO, owners, steps) and review it after each exercise.
